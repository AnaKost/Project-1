Phobos

This moon orbits only a few thousand miles above Mars' surface. Its proximity to Mars is one of the reasons why astronomers were unable to see the satellite until the late 19th century. In fact Phobos is getting closer to Mars over the centuries and will eventually breakup or get pulled into the Martian surface. Images from the Mariner 9 spacecraft revealed the both Phobos and Demios have lumpy potato-like shapes rather than the spherical shape like most moons. Observations of Phobos were limited by the tidal locking of the moon to the planet, resulting in the same side always facing outward.

Doomed Moon

Phobos won't orbit around Mars forever. The "doomed"moon is spiraling inward at a rate of 1.8 centimeters per year. Within 50 million years, the moon will either collide with its planet, or it will be torn into rubble and scattered as a ring around Mars. The unusual characteristics of Phobos including its decaying orbit led some scientists in the 1950's and 1960's to conclude that it was artificial. Further examination revealed that the moon bears stronger resemblance to a rubble pile than an artificial satellite.
